I.

Scene One — The Courtyard off Dongsi

The summer heat has finally broken.

It broke the way Peking summers always do — not gradually, not with warning, but overnight, as if some bureaucrat in heaven stamped a document and the season simply ended. The hutongs smell of wood smoke and ripe persimmon now. The coal sellers are already stacking their winter stores in the lanes behind Fengsheng. Women are out in the mornings with padded jackets over their shoulders, not yet wearing them, just carrying them, the way the body hedges its bets between seasons.

It is the third of September, 1923. Two days ago, a telegram arrived at the British Legation. The news has been spreading through Peking's small foreign community in the way that catastrophic news always spreads — in whispers at the club, then louder at dinner parties, then in the Chinese press, and finally in black ink on the broadsheets pasted to the walls of every pailou gate in the city:

GREAT EARTHQUAKE DEVASTATES TOKYO AND YOKOHAMA. 140,000 FEARED DEAD.

The number doesn't fit in the mind properly. It sits there, wrong-shaped.

The Ch'ien household occupies the west wing of a courtyard compound on a hutong off Dongsi — three rooms, a narrow courtyard with a pomegranate tree that produces fruit too sour to eat but too beautiful to cut down, and a small study where the lamp burns most nights past midnight. The compound belongs to an elderly bannerman family who rents the wing to faculty. The rent is paid irregularly by the university administration, a fact Chih-yüan has elected not to investigate too closely.

This morning, Hélène has commandeered the study table. Her notebooks are open — three of them, in various stages of incompletion — beside a typed copy of the expedition briefing that Professor Wang Enlai's secretary delivered to the door on Friday. She has a cigarette going in the brass ashtray that she bought at Liulichang because it was shaped like a fish and she found it aesthetically interesting. The cigarette is mostly burning itself.

She hasn't looked up in twenty minutes.

The briefing document bears the seal of Peking University's Department of Geology and the countersignature of the Geological Survey of China. It is titled, with characteristic academic understatement:

Preliminary Research Expedition to the Caves of the Thousand Buddhas, Dunhuang, Gansu Province, and Associated Sites Along the Western Corridor: September–December 1923.

It is thirty-one pages long, including appendices.

On the cover, someone — Wang, his handwriting is distinctive — has added a note in ink:

In Wang Enlai's hand, on the cover

"The American, Warner, has been allocated four rooms at the Wagon-Lits. He will wish to meet the full team before the fifteenth. I have told him Dr. Ch'ien is the lead Chinese scientific researcher on this expedition. He was very polite about this. Please let me know your thoughts on the briefing when you have had time. — W.E."

The pomegranate tree in the courtyard drops a fruit. It lands with a soft thud on the paving stones. Neither of them moves to retrieve it.

The morning light through the paper windows is the particular gold of early September in North China — thin, clear, without the summer's humidity. From somewhere down the hutong, a vendor is calling his wares. The syllables carry without the words.

She is reading the appendix on associated sites — a list of coordinates and grid references that Warner's team has supplied, cross-referenced with Stein's 1907 survey maps. The names are transliterated inconsistently across three different romanization systems, which she has begun correcting in pencil in the margins.

Chih-yüan sets down his tea and asks, in French:

"Tes pensées?" ("Your thoughts?")

She looks up. Not at him — past him, at the middle distance that means she is arranging something in her mind.

She taps the appendix. The grid references Warner's team supplied cluster around Dunhuang in the expected way, but three of them do not. They are further west, further south, in a region the Stein maps label only with topographic notations and no site names at all.

Chih-yüan considers this for a moment — not the answer, which he knows, but the phrasing of it. He has learned to hear what his wife is actually asking. She is not asking whether Warner objects. She is asking whether he had to argue.

Outside, the pomegranate tree drops a fruit onto the paving stones. Neither of them moves. The coal seller's call echoes down the hutong, and then fades, and the courtyard is quiet again in the thin September gold.

"À trois heures," Chih-yüan says eventually. ("At three o'clock.")

The morning moves. Somewhere across the city, in the lane behind the North China Union Language School, Marie Boucher is returning from the morning market with a cloth bag of autumn vegetables — she has found that doing something with her hands helps her Mandarin come more naturally, and the vegetable sellers do not mind being practiced on. And in his quarters in the university staff residences — one room, very clean, a locked trunk under the bed, a Gutka on the table beside the lamp — Gurdit Singh is reading the expedition briefing for the third time. He has underlined nothing. He never underlines. He reads until he remembers.

II.

Gurdit's Room — Preliminary Assessment

Keeper note — confirmed canon

Gurdit was not the original security appointment. The original man — let's say a Mr. Fenton, a British ex-pat who had done two previous Survey trips along the western corridor and came recommended by the legation — fell ill in late August. Dysentery, probably, the kind that doesn't kill you but leaves you in no condition to travel for six weeks minimum. Wang contacted Gurdit ten days ago. The handoff was abrupt. Gurdit has had the briefing materials for eight days.

He has read them thoroughly. He has questions.

His room in the university staff residences faces east. The morning light comes in clean and early, which suits him. He has been awake since before dawn — the nitnem completed in the dark, the five prayers said quietly in Punjabi into the still air of the room, the Gutka closed and set back on the table with both hands.

By the time the light came up, he was already at the desk.

The briefing document is thirty-one pages. His copy has no marks in it. What it has instead is a separate sheet of paper — his own, plain white, university stock taken from the supply cabinet in Wang's outer office — on which he has been writing in a small, very neat hand. English on the left margin. Punjabi annotations on the right where the English runs out or where a thought is private.

The list, as it stands this morning:

Expedition Security — Preliminary Assessment
G. S. Bajwa, Director of Expedition Security · Draft — for personal use / meeting preparation

Personnel

  • Full party composition not confirmed in writing. Briefing names Dr. Ch'ien and Mr. Warner's team. No formal list of porters, drivers, or local guides yet supplied. Fenton's notes (if any exist) not yet transferred to me. Query Wang: where are Fenton's field notes?
  • Medical officer listed as Miss Boucher, nurse, North China Union Language School. Credentials appear adequate for field conditions. Have not yet met her. Reserve judgment.
  • Warner's team: four names listed in appendix C, roles unspecified beyond "research assistants." Need to know which of these men has field experience and which does not. A scholar who has not carried his own pack is a liability in the Gansu corridor.

Route

  • Peking to Zhengzhou by rail: no concerns.
  • Zhengzhou to Lanzhou: rail to Baoji, then road. The road from Baoji west through the Wei River valley has seen bandit activity since at least 1921. Briefing does not mention this. Fenton may have had contacts in Baoji who could advise — again, need his notes.
  • Lanzhou to Dunhuang: this is the critical stretch. 1,200 li of Gansu corridor. The briefing proposes a mixed camel-and-cart arrangement through a merchant intermediary in Lanzhou named Mr. Liu Dequan. I do not know this man. His references are not included. Who recommended him? On what basis?
  • Water sources along the Gansu corridor route: the briefing cites Stein's 1907 survey. Stein traveled sixteen years ago. Water sources shift. I need current intelligence. Query: does the Geological Survey have men who have traveled this corridor in the last three years?

Equipment

  • Firearms: briefing does not address this directly. I will carry my own. What is the party's situation? A four-month expedition through bandit country requires at minimum that the security director is not the only armed person in the party. Raise at meeting — carefully.
  • Medical supplies: see Miss Boucher. Does she have a supply list? Has anyone consulted her on what the route requires specifically? Gansu corridor in autumn: cold injury, respiratory illness from the dust, gastric illness from water sources. Snakebite less likely at this season but not impossible in the lower elevations.
  • The three supplementary sites (W-7, W-8, W-11): no logistical information provided. If these sites require deviation from the main Dunhuang route, the caravan arrangement must account for it. At present it cannot, because I have no coordinates beyond the grid references and no description of terrain, access, or distance from the main road. This is a significant omission.

Timing

  • Departure proposed 17 or 18 September. Eight days from now if the earlier date holds, nine if the later.
  • Caravan animals must be arranged in Lanzhou, not Peking — correct. But confirmation from Liu Dequan that animals are reserved and ready should have been sent already. Has it? Query Wang.
  • The Dunhuang sites should be reached before the end of October if we are to have adequate working time before winter closes the corridor. Margin is thin. Any significant delay en route — banditry, vehicle failure, illness — and we lose working days at the site.

Open Questions for the Meeting

  1. Fenton's notes — where are they, and can I have them today?
  2. Liu Dequan — full reference and confirmation of caravan booking.
  3. Warner's "research assistants" — which of them has field experience?
  4. W-7, W-8, W-11 — what are these sites, what is the access, and why were they omitted from the briefing body?
  5. Firearms for the party — what is the current situation and what can be arranged in the time remaining?

He sets down his pen. Reads the list through once. His Punjabi annotations in the right margin are not translated here — they are his own business — but they are not angry. They are the notes of a man who has assessed a situation with more gaps than he would prefer, and who is deciding, methodically, what can still be made right and what must simply be managed.

Outside his window, the university grounds are coming to life. A student crossing the courtyard below, books under his arm. The smell of coal smoke from the kitchen block. Somewhere, a bicycle bell.

He has eight days. Perhaps nine.

He folds the list once, lengthwise, and tucks it into the inside pocket of his jacket.

RollGurdit — Navigate (60) — Regular

He has been tracing the Gansu corridor route on the briefing's map with his finger for the third morning running. The map is Stein's, redrawn and reduced. Something about the distance between the last marked water source and the approach to Dunhuang is not sitting right with him. He cannot yet say whether this is the map's error, Stein's, or his own unfamiliarity with the terrain.

Whether his instinct is correct — we'll know when you roll.

6 — Extreme Success

The map is wrong.

Not Stein's map — Stein's original survey is meticulous, the man was nothing if not meticulous — but the redrawing. Someone at the Survey, working from Stein's published plates and reducing them to fit the briefing's page dimensions, has introduced an error in the scaling of the final leg. The distance between the last marked well at Anxi and the approach road to the Mogao caves has been compressed. On the briefing map it reads as perhaps two days' travel with laden camels. Gurdit's eye, running the scale bar against his thumb and then against the corridor's known dimensions from the surrounding geography, puts it closer to three and a half.

A day and a half. With laden camels in autumn, in the Gansu corridor, that is not a rounding error. That is the difference between arriving at Dunhuang with animals that can still work and arriving with animals that need four days' rest before they are useful.

He sits with this for a moment.

Then he picks up his pen and writes at the bottom of his list, in English, underlined once:

Anxi to Dunhuang: verify actual distance. Briefing map scaling error — last leg approx. 1.5 days longer than shown. Caravan water supply must be recalculated accordingly. Raise with Wang before meeting if possible.

And beneath that, in Punjabi, smaller:

ਫੈਂਟਨ ਨੇ ਇਹ ਦੇਖਿਆ ਹੋਣਾ ਚਾਹੀਦਾ ਸੀ।
(Fenton should have seen this.)

He does not write what follows from that thought, which is: and if Fenton did see it and said nothing, I want to know why, and if Fenton did not see it, then Wang has lost something more significant than a security director.

He unfolds his list, adds the Anxi note to the ROUTE section where it belongs, refolds it, and puts it back in his jacket.

The morning bell sounds from the university's main building. He has perhaps two hours before he should present himself at Wang's office — early enough to speak privately before the three o'clock meeting, late enough not to seem rattled.

He is not rattled. But he would like Fenton's notes.

He would like them very much.

III.

Marie's Room, and the Day's Sequence

Marie's room at the North China Union Language School is on the second floor of the women's residential building, east-facing, small enough that the two beds and two desks leave a corridor between them just wide enough to pass through sideways if the other person stands still. The window looks out over the school's back garden, which in September is still producing the last of its beans, tended by the groundskeeper's wife and two of the older students who have taken it upon themselves to learn gardening vocabulary by doing.

Phyllis Joan Weatherby is sitting on her bed with her Bible open on her knees when Marie comes back from the washroom. She is twenty-four, pink-cheeked, from Victoria by way of a Methodist mission training college in Melbourne, and she has the particular quality of cheerfulness that is entirely genuine and occasionally exhausting. She is reading — her lips move slightly — and does not look up until Marie begins pulling her good blouse from the wardrobe.

"You're dressed early," Phyllis says, in English. Her accent makes early into two distinct syllables.

"I have a long day." Marie folds the blouse over her arm and considers the room, doing the morning's small inventory: what needs doing, in what order, how much time each step will take. "I should have told you last night, but you were already asleep. I'm leaving the school. Not immediately — but soon. I've been asked to serve as medical officer on a research expedition. West. Gansu Province, possibly further."

Phyllis looks up from her Bible.

"West," she repeats.

"The Caves of the Thousand Buddhas, to start. Then perhaps further along the corridor. I'll know more after today's meeting."

There is a pause in which Phyllis processes this. She is not a woman who processes quickly, but she processes honestly, which Marie has come to appreciate.

"How long?"

"Four months, approximately. Through December."

"Marie." Phyllis sets the Bible down on the bed beside her. "That's — are you — is it safe?"

"There's a security director. A Mr. Singh, Gurdit Singh Bajwa — a retired Subedar, apparently, Peking University's man. I'm told he's quite capable." She hasn't met him. She is told. She keeps this distinction quiet. "And it's a proper academic expedition, not a commercial venture. Geologists and linguists."

"Linguists," Phyllis says, with the slight doubt of someone who is not sure linguists count.

"And I'll have the work. Real work." She says this simply, without drama, and Phyllis hears it the way it is meant — not as a criticism of the language school's charity clinic rotation, which is perfectly decent, but as a statement of fact about what Marie came to China for. Phyllis has her own version of the same feeling. She recognizes it.

"Right," Phyllis says. "Well. I'll pray for you."

"I'd be grateful." Marie means it.

The day's sequence, as she has arranged it in her head while Phyllis finishes her devotionals:

First: the Mission.

Dean Calder at the Presbyterian mission on Dengshikou Street. She needs his list — the stations and contacts along the Gansu corridor and beyond where a Presbyterian missionary in good standing might find shelter, send a wire, or access emergency funds. The mission society has maintained this network since the 1890s. She has heard it described as more reliable than the telegraph in some stretches of the interior. She wants it in her hand before she leaves Peking.

She also needs his signature on her leave-of-absence paperwork, which she has drafted herself and which is, she is fairly certain, correctly done. Dean Calder will read it with the expression he reserves for things that are technically correct but that he wishes were otherwise.

Second: the PUMC.

Anna Wolf at the Peking Union Medical College is expecting her by ten. The Rockefeller money that built PUMC has also funded a modest supply allocation for mission-affiliated medical workers taking postings in the interior — a recognition that the interior is where the patients are and the drugs are not. Anna has been assembling Marie's expedition kit for the past week, based on the route information Marie sent her.

She does not yet know what Anna has included. She has a list of what she asked for. These two lists will not be identical. They never are.

Third: the university.

Three o'clock. She will bring the medical crates — however many there are — to the meeting at Peking University's Geology Building, where she will hand them to a man she has never met.

Gurdit Singh Bajwa.

She has looked up the name. She knows what a Subedar is. She knows, because her sister Thérèse wrote it in a letter two years ago in the context of a longer letter about the Ghadar movement and the Komagata Maru, what a Jat Sikh is. She knows that the Komagata Maru's Baba Gurdit Singh is not, presumably, this Gurdit Singh, but that they share a name and a faith and a Punjab that has been treated in particular ways by an empire that both she and he have reasons to think about carefully.

She puts on her coat. She picks up her bag. She pauses at the door.

"Phyllis," she says.

Phyllis looks up.

"The beans," Marie says. "The garden. I've been doing Wednesday mornings with Mrs. Huang. Could you—"

"Yes," Phyllis says, before she finishes. "Of course."

Marie nods. She goes.

The hutong outside the school's back gate smells of coal smoke and something frying — the breakfast stall on the corner, where a man with a cart sells jianbing to students and early office workers. Marie has eaten. She keeps walking.

The morning is cold enough for her breath to show.

IV.

The Geology Building, Peking University — 3:00 PM

The afternoon light has shifted. Time moves.

The room Wang Enlai uses for departmental meetings is on the second floor of the Geology Building, which smells, as all geology buildings do, of mineral dust and old paper and something faintly sulfurous from the sample cabinets along the east wall. The table is mahogany, scarred by decades of specimen trays and equipment cases set down without care. Six chairs around it. A blackboard on the north wall with a map of the Gansu corridor chalked on it in Wang's precise hand, the major waypoints labeled in both Chinese characters and English.

The window looks west. The afternoon light comes in at a low angle, September-thin, laying a bar of gold across the table's middle.

Wang Enlai is already standing when they arrive. He is fifty-one, slight, with the scholar's slight stoop that comes from decades over a desk, and he wears his Western suit with the particular ease of a man who has worn Western suits long enough to forget he is wearing one. His English is Oxford-accented and precise. He shakes hands with each of them in turn — Chih-yüan first, then Hélène with a small bow, then Marie, and then Gurdit, whom he greets with both hands and something warmer than the others got:

"Gurdit Singh-ji. I am glad you are here. I am sorry the circumstances were not better."

"The circumstances are what they are, Professor," Gurdit says. "I have questions."

"I expected nothing less." Wang gestures to the chairs. "Please. All of you."

They settle. The four investigators on one side of the table — not quite by design, but it happens that way, Chih-yüan and Hélène taking the middle chairs and Marie and Gurdit anchoring the ends, with perhaps six inches more space between Gurdit and Marie than between the married couple. Wang takes the chair opposite Chih-yüan. He has a leather folder open in front of him.

He looks at them for a moment — the four of them, assembled — and something in his expression suggests that he finds them, collectively, both more and less than he expected.

"Good," he says, quietly, as if confirming something to himself.

Then, in a brisker register:

"Warner meets us on the seventh. That gives us four days before we must present a unified picture to the American. I would like us to have a unified picture before then. So — questions, concerns, observations. I have my own list. But I would rather hear yours first."

He folds his hands on the table.

The bar of afternoon light lies across the map of the Gansu corridor on the blackboard. The last marked water source before Dunhuang is labeled An-hsi — Anxi — in Wang's chalk script, and it is exactly as far from the Mogao caves as the briefing map suggests.

Which is to say: not far enough.

Gurdit has his folded paper in his inside pocket. He has not taken it out yet.

He is waiting to see who speaks first.

Marie's concerns, identified during the tram ride from the PUMC to the university, the crates stacked in the luggage rack above her with a student holding one steady on the curves:

She is thinking about the route. Gansu corridor, autumn into winter, high desert, altitude gain as they move west. The kit Anna assembled is a good trauma kit and a reasonable general kit. What it is not — what she is not certain it is — is a kit calibrated to the specific disease environment of the western corridor in this season.

She knows what she knows. She trained at Vancouver General, which is an excellent hospital. She has been in Peking seven months and has seen the charity patients at the PUMC clinic: tuberculosis, gastric illness, the usual catalogue of urban poverty medicine. She has not been west of Peking.

The question she cannot yet answer from her own knowledge: what does she not know to ask for?

94 — Failure (the right kind)

A 94 is a failure — and the right kind of failure for this moment. She has looked at the kit carefully and found it satisfactory. It is satisfactory. Anna Wolf is good at her job. The crates are well-packed, the substitutions are defensible, the cold-injury additions are sensible.

Marie does not know about kala-azar — the leishmaniasis that moves through parts of the Gansu corridor, slow and wasting, that looks like nothing for weeks before it doesn't. She does not know that the dust of the Taklamakan carries a particular fungal load in the autumn that produces a respiratory condition the local population call sha fei, sand lung, that is not pneumonia and does not respond to the treatments for pneumonia. She does not know that the wells between Anxi and Dunhuang are, in a dry year, occasionally alkaline enough to cause serious gastric damage even when boiled — and this has been a dry year.

She doesn't know any of this. The kit looks complete to her.

She arrives at the university at ten minutes before three, the crates stacked on a borrowed handcart that a young groundskeeper is pulling for her across the courtyard. She thanks him in Mandarin — xièxie nín (謝謝您) — and he looks pleased and surprised in the way that people do when a foreigner uses the polite form without prompting.

She leaves the crates in the corridor outside Wang's meeting room, stacked neatly against the wall. She will raise them when the moment is right.

She goes in. She finds three people already seated and a fourth — the man at the end of the table, large, turbaned, with a scar through his left eyebrow and a folded piece of paper in front of him that he has not yet opened — who looks at her when she enters with the measuring attention of a man doing a professional assessment and finding the subject not what he expected, without yet deciding whether that is good or bad.

She takes the chair at her end of the table.

He gives her a small, formal nod.

She returns it.

Wang says: "Good. Now we are all here."

The Library Cave

Something shifts.

It comes back the way suppressed things do — not gradually but all at once, like a word you couldn't find for an hour arriving the moment you stop reaching for it. The Library Cave. IX.

The Caves of the Thousand Buddhas

The valley announces itself before the caves do — a fold in the desert that has no business being there, trees stripped bare by October, ravens watching from every branch. Then the cliff face, honeycombed with dark openings tier upon tier, ladders connecting them like the rigging of some improbable ship run aground in the Gobi.

At the base of the cliff, a small figure in Taoist robes is waiting. Wang Yuanlu is perhaps seventy-five, slight, with the unhurried stillness of a man who has been in one place long enough that he no longer needs to perform composure. He watches the cars stop. He watches them climb out. His eyes move to Warner and then away, with the particular quality of a man who has already taken that particular measurement and filed it.

他说，用普通话，对着整个队伍而不是任何一个人： ("He speaks, in Mandarin, to the group rather than to any one person:") "你们来了很久了。" ("You have been a long time coming.")

It is not a complaint. It is simply the shape of things, stated plainly, by a man who has learned to watch Westerners arrive and take note of what they do next.

Chih-yüan steps forward. He inclines his head at the correct depth for a man of Wang Yuanlu's age and position — not a bow, not a nod, something precisely calibrated between them — and introduces himself, in Mandarin, as a researcher from Peking University, attached to the expedition in a scientific capacity. He names the others briefly and accurately. He does not mention Warner.

"道长，" ("Daoist Master,") he says, "我们很荣幸能来拜访。" ("We are honored to visit.")

Wang Yuanlu looks at him for a long moment with eyes that have seen forty years of desert light and have not softened for it. Then something in his face shifts, fractionally.

"錢伯驤，" he says. Not a question — he has the name already, from Wang Enlai presumably, and has taken the trouble to remember it. "你的老师是个好人。" ("Your teacher is a good man.")

He turns and begins walking toward the caves, expecting them to follow.

The caves are cooler than the desert, the rock holding the night's cold well into the afternoon. Wang Yuanlu leads them along the base of the cliff without commentary, past cave mouths dark and open, past the ladders rising to the upper tiers. The murals visible in the nearest openings are extraordinary even in passing — figures in gold and ochre and lapis, a blue that has no business surviving a thousand years in this light.

Some of it has not survived. The damage, when it appears, is unmistakeable — a different kind of mark than age or weather. Soldiers had been here, and soldiers leave a particular signature.

Wang Yuanlu does not point any of this out. He walks past it with the composure of a man who has already done his grieving.

He shows them to a large ground-level cave set aside for their quarters. The walls here are painted floor to ceiling — Buddhas, bodhisattvas, donors in Tang-dynasty silks, a heaven populated with musicians. Several of the human figures have been retouched in a paler pigment, the restoration work inexpert but earnest.

The painted eyes follow them as they move through the space.

Wang Yuanlu stops at the entrance and looks at Chih-yüan.

"你们需要什么，就来找我。" ("Whatever you need, come to me.") A pause, precise and deliberate. "来找我。不是他。" ("Come to me. Not him.")

X.

Gates That Should Not Be Opened

Hélène steps forward and repeats the phrase in Mandarin, carefully, watching his face.

"他们来了。末日降临。因为他们来了。"

Wang Yuanlu is still for a moment that goes on slightly longer than a moment should.

Then he looks at her with an attention that is different in quality from how he looked at her before.

"你在哪里看到这个的？" ("Where did you see this?")

Wang Yuanlu looks at Hélène for a long moment. Then he turns and walks deeper into the cave complex without answering.

At the first bend he stops, his back still to them, and says quietly:

"来。" ("Come.")

Wang Yuanlu does not look back to see who is behind him. He walks as though the question of who follows has already been settled to his satisfaction.

Hélène follows. Chih-yüan moves after her without hesitation and the old priest does not break stride, which is its own kind of permission.

The cave narrows briefly and then opens into a smaller chamber, this one less damaged than the others. The murals here are Tang — eighth century, perhaps earlier — and largely intact. A bodhisattva in white regards them from the far wall with an expression of absolute equanimity.

Wang Yuanlu stops beneath it. He does not look at the painting. He looks at Hélène.

"这句话，" ("This phrase,") he says, "在这里已经存在了很久。" ("has existed here for a very long time.") He pauses. "比俄国人早得多。" ("Much longer than the Russians.")

He lets that settle.

"你是怎么知道的？" ("How do you know it?")

"谁是'他们'？" ("Who are 'they'?") Hélène asks.

Wang Yuanlu regards her for a long moment. His eyes move briefly to Chih-yüan, then back.

"有些门，" ("There are gates,") he says finally, "不应该被打开。" ("that should not be opened.")

He looks up at the bodhisattva on the wall.

"'他们'是那些不同意的人。" ("'They' are those who disagree.")

Chih-yüan: "你在守护这些门吗？" ("Do you stand watch at the gates?")

Wang Yuanlu looks at him for the first time with something other than assessment.

"我们都是。" ("We all do.")

A pause.

"有些人不知道自己在守护。" ("Some do not know they are guarding.")

Wang Yuanlu does not say anything further. He turns and walks back toward the light, his robes whispering on the cave floor, and leaves them alone with the bodhisattva.

XI.

The Painted Eyes, and the Dawn

That night the four of them eat together in their quarters, the lamp throwing the painted figures into unfamiliar relief. The musicians on the ceiling appear to move when the flame gutters. Warner eats with his students next door. Through the wall, occasionally, his voice — explaining something, correcting something.

The painted eyes watch the room.

Chih-yüan lies awake for a time after the lamp is out. In the dark the cave smells of mineral dust and something older — incense, perhaps, worked so deeply into the rock over so many centuries that it has become the rock.

He thinks about the phrase some do not know they are guarding.

He thinks about the tracing in his field journal — the symbol from Fenton's map, the three characters beneath it in no recognized script. He has not shown it to anyone.

He does not take it out now.

The cold comes before the light. Marie is already outside when the sky begins to pale, her breath misting, the Lee-Enfield broken down on its cloth on a flat rock. Gurdit emerges a few minutes later with two cups of tea and sets one beside her without comment.

The ravens watch from the trees.

By the time Hélène and Chih-yüan are up, Warner is already on the ladders above them, moving between the upper caves with a lantern, his red hair visible against the cliff face. His students are not yet awake.

Wang Yuanlu is at the base of the cliff, watching him.
