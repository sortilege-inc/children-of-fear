The Gate
The Tokabhaya's gates destroy. A marriage across two faiths would generate.
Underneath everything, the campaign is asking: what is a gate for?
Fear against love — in the most literal Lovecraftian sense

The Children of Fear is a horror campaign, but for this party it runs a second engine alongside the Mythos plot — and the two are in dialogue. Both are about thresholds: what should and should not be opened, and what happens when incommensurables meet. This is where those threads are kept.

The Ch'ien Marriage 錢氏婚姻

Chih-yüan and Hélène are deeply, genuinely married — the party's foundational bond and its most stable attachment. They argue well about politics; it is their favorite Sunday afternoon. And they carry, negotiated openly and years ago, a don't-ask-don't-tell arrangement. Her erotic appetites significantly outstrip his, and her honesty made secret affairs impossible; he agreed out of a patient temperament, a modest appetite, a cultural familiarity with marriage and eros as separate categories, and the understanding that forcing fidelity would only make a worse marriage.

His love shows as careful attention rather than possession; her gratitude runs under everything she does. The cost is real but small and steady — untested through the quiet Peking years. The expedition is what tests it. Some nights he lies awake weighing what the arrangement has cost him and what it has given him.

Play his restraint as strength

He knows before Hélène admits it to herself that her attention has pivoted. He is not oblivious — he is choosing not to know the details. The marriage reads as real and loving at all times.

Gurdit & Marie 緩燃

The campaign's central interpersonal arc, and its slowest burn. Gurdit and Marie are drawn to each other and locked in a trap of mutual respect, each disciplined into restraint. The Komagata Maru — which happened in Marie's country, and shares a name with her bodyguard — sits unspoken between them. They recognize each other's silences.

The Geometry, at the Start

Hélène → Gurdit The initial pull Magnetically drawn to his banked physical intensity — a contrast to her gentle, scholarly husband — but self-aware about the colonial dynamic. Her approach is watchful, never presuming.
Gurdit → Marie The quiet turn A five-year widower, drawn not to a type but to a fellow carrier of grief with dignity and a serious, examined faith. He does nothing overt and notices everything — whether she ate, when her hands are cold.
Marie → restraint The held line She recognizes the pull and holds it, for three reasons: her mission board's rules against entanglement, her Komagata-Maru-informed sense of the colonial weight, and — the quiet one — a fear of her own capacity for wanting something wholly.

The Arc

The Recalibration. About two weeks in, Hélène's anthropologist's eye notices that Gurdit's attention is on Marie, not on her. She is not hurt — she is fascinated — and she redirects her energy into helping Marie articulate her own interior life.

The Conversations. Over weeks, in three registers: scholarly (the Song of Songs, Bernard of Clairvaux, the mystical traditions — showing Marie that her tradition's silences are not its only shape); anthropological (how communities arrange erotic life, and, carefully, the shape of Hélène's own marriage); and personal, only if Marie opens the door. They are about Marie — never about Gurdit. Hélène will not push, will not matchmake, will not engineer.

The Marriage Frame 婚姻之框

Late — after Marie has been thinking seriously about her own feelings — Hélène offers not a solution but a frame. Her insight: a marriage would let Gurdit and Marie each practice the deepest virtues of their own faith toward the other, without either converting or syncretizing. Both traditions have serious theologies of marriage — the Sikh Anand Karaj and its four laavan; the Presbyterian understanding of marriage as vocation. The practical shape is both, and separately: a full Anand Karaj and a Presbyterian blessing; neither converts.

The frame dissolves Marie's vocation-versus-desire dilemma. Marriage is itself a Christian vocation; returning to Vancouver's Chinese community as an interfaith Asian-Canadian family would fulfill her mission, not abandon it. And it echoes what her father Halvor already did — a man who gave up his church and his patronymic to marry across a line. Saying yes to Gurdit would be continuing her family's tradition, not betraying it.

Gurdit's reception

He hears it from Marie, not from Hélène. Undone quietly, he takes it to dawn prayers and re-reads the laavan; Sikh widowhood theology permits remarriage, so he feels no guilt. He writes to his parents in Punjab for their blessing. Their reply arrives months later, in Tibet — cautious but glad: their son has been alone too long.

Where It Can Go

  • Integration. Marie keeps her vocation but matures — the pull acknowledged, absorbed, and carried.
  • Acknowledgment. A single confession, most likely in Tibet after a near-death encounter, and then a lifetime of respectful letters.
  • The Choice. The dual marriage of Hélène's frame — socially catastrophic, entirely possible; the Vancouver bridge-family version the most powerful of all.
  • Loss. The Mythos takes one, or both. Fear wins the argument with love.

The Man at the Center 樞紐

For all that the romance belongs to Gurdit and Marie, its stability rests on Chih-yüan. That Gurdit's interest landed on Marie rather than on Hélène is, to him, a small relief he does not name — it protects the marriage's arrangement and, just as much, his own growing friendship with Gurdit. He becomes, quietly, the campaign's keeper of emotional secrets: the man at the center of every fault line, his POW 90 giving him the soul-weight to hold it. His own quiet loneliness inside a marriage he chose gives him a particular insight into everyone else's.

The thematic mirror

The Tokabhaya mean to open the Gates of Agartha — a catastrophic opening. Hélène's proposed marriage is the generative opening of a gate between two people separated by the structures of their lives. Fear against love, in the most literal Lovecraftian sense. What is a gate for?

A note on pacing

Do not resolve early. Consummation in Chapter Three kills the thread; never acknowledging it kills the payoff. The target is late acknowledgment — Tibet, or a letter after the campaign ends. The pressure is generative because it is constrained.
